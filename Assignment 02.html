Bhagat Singh

A hero of the early 20th-century Indian independence movement.



Bhagat Singh was a prominent Indian revolutionary and freedom fighter who played a key role in the struggle for India’s independence from British rule. Born on September 28, 1907, in Banga, Punjab (now in Pakistan), he became a symbol of resistance against colonial oppression.

Bhagat Singh is best known for his involvement in the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA) and his radical approach to achieving independence. He was deeply influenced by the writings and ideologies of Marxism and was committed to the idea of a socialist revolution in India.

One of his most notable actions was the bombing of the Central Legislative Assembly in Delhi in 1929, along with Batukeshwar Dutt. The intent was to protest against repressive laws and to make a political statement. They were arrested and used their trial as a platform to propagate their ideas of revolution and independence.

Bhagat Singh was also involved in the killing of British police officer John Saunders in 1928, which he and his associates carried out in retaliation for the death of Lala Lajpat Rai, who had succumbed to injuries inflicted by the police during a protest.

His trial and subsequent execution on March 23, 1931, at the age of 23, turned him into a martyr and a national hero. Bhagat Singh’s courage, revolutionary zeal, and his ultimate sacrifice continue to inspire many in India and beyond, symbolizing the struggle for justice and freedom.

Here’s a time line of Bhagat Singh’s life:
Early Life
  • 1907- Bhagat Singh is born in Banga, Punjab (now in Pakistan), into a Sikh family with a history of political activism.
  • 1919- The Jallianwala Bagh massacre occurs, deeply impacting young Bhagat Singh and fueling his desire for independence.
Formative Years
  • 1920- Bhagat Singh joins the freedom movement and becomes influenced by the teachings of revolutionaries like Lala Lajpat Rai and leaders such as Subhas Chandra Bose.
  • 1923- He moves to Lahore to study at the National College, where he becomes involved in revolutionary activities and joins the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA).
Revolutionary Activities
  • 1928- Bhagat Singh and his associates plan to protest against the Simon Commission, which is seen as a symbol of British oppression. During the protest, Lala Lajpat Rai is injured and later dies due to the injuries. In retaliation, Bhagat Singh and his group plot to kill James A. Scott, whom they mistakenly identify as responsible for Rai’s death, but end up killing J.P. Saunders instead.
  • 1929- Bhagat Singh and Batukeshwar Dutt throw non-lethal bombs in the Central Legislative Assembly in Delhi to protest against repressive laws. They are arrested and use the trial as a platform to promote their revolutionary ideals.
Arrest and Trial
  • 1930- Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Sukhdev are sentenced to death for their involvement in the killing of J.P. Saunders. Their trial garners widespread attention and sympathy across India and abroad.
  • March 23,1931- Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Sukhdev are executed in Lahore Central Jail.
Legacy:

Bhagat Singh's legacy as a martyr and revolutionary continues to inspire generations. His writings and sacrifice are celebrated in India as a symbol of courage and resistance against colonial rule. Bhagat Singh's life, though short, had a profound impact on the Indian independence movement and he remains a revered figure in Indian history.
